Welcome to Memory is a Lie

We like to believe that our memories are objective, like a video camera recording everything we see and hear. But research in psychology and neuroscience tells a different story. Memory is not a passive archive, but is actually an active reconstruction. It is shaped by expectations, associations, distractions, and context. This means that memory is not always reliable.

Our website, Memory is a Lie, invites you to experience this firsthand. Through four engaging, science-based games, you'll see the limitations of your short-term memory, how your memory can distort reality, and even create entirely false recollections. Each game is paired with a clear explanation of the underlying memory science, a real-life example showing how it impacts the world, and a brief reflection to help you connect your experience to your own life. Whether you're a student, a teacher, or just someone curious about the mind, you'll find something to learn and be surprised by engaging with this site.

Unlike brain-training platforms focused on self-optimization, our aim is understanding. In an age of misinformation, overstimulation, and overreliance on testimony, knowing how memory works is crucial not only academically, but socially, legally, and politically as well.

Memory Topic: Working Memory and Miller’s Law
This game is based on George A. Miller’s 1956 paper, “The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two,” which suggests that the average person can hold about seven items (plus or minus two) in their short-term memory. Working memory is your mind’s “scratchpad”, or a place where information is temporarily stored and manipulated. It’s crucial for mental math, following directions, and holding conversations. “Number Crunch” highlights just how narrow this window is, especially when you’re under pressure or distracted. It also reveals a central truth about memory: working memory is less about storage capacity and more about managing attention and limitations.


Real-World Case Study: Memory in Emergency Communication
In high-stakes settings like 911 dispatch or military command, messages must be short, repeatable, and easily remembered. That’s because human working memory has strict limits. If a firefighter hears a complex address or a multi-step instruction all at once, the consequences of forgetting can be serious. This is why communication training emphasizes chunking and simplicity. The key lesson here is that less is often more when memory is involved!

Memory Topic: Visual-Spatial Working Memory
This game taps into your visuo-spatial sketchpad which is a component of working memory that holds mental images and spatial relationships. This is what helps you visualize a map in your head, remember where you left your bag, or picture how to rearrange furniture. While we often think of memory as verbal or narrative, spatial memory is just as vital and again, and just as fallible.


Real-World Case Study: London Taxi Drivers and The Brain
A famous study by neuroscientist Eleanor Maguire found that London taxi drivers, who must memorize thousands of street names and routes, had larger posterior hippocampi, which is the brain region linked to spatial navigation, than the average citizen. Yet, even these taxi drivers sometimes take wrong turns under stress. Similarly, pilots, surgeons, and athletes all depend on spatial memory, which can fail under pressure or fatigue. “Location Recall” simulates those challenges in miniature, helping you experience both mastery and mental overload.

Memory Topic: The DRM Paradigm and Constructive Memory
This game is based on the Deese-Roediger-McDermott (DRM) paradigm, a classic experiment that reveals how our brains create memories that feel real but aren’t. Rather than recalling exact inputs, our minds reconstruct meaning by filling in likely but false associations. The closer a word is to a theme, the more your brain wants to believe it was present. This process is usually adaptive and it helps us generalize and infer but it’s also vulnerable to distortion. This is constructive memory in action: not storage, but storytelling.


Real-World Case Study: Jennifer Thompson and Ronald Cotton
Jennifer Thompson, a North Carolina college student, was convinced she had correctly identified her rapist in a police lineup. Her memory seemed vivid and certain. But years later, DNA evidence proved Ronald Cotton was innocent. Thompson had fallen prey to false memory and it was reinforced by suggestion and confidence. Cases like this are not rare. The Innocence Project estimates that eyewitness misidentification contributes to about 70% of wrongful convictions overturned by DNA evidence. This game educates on how such tragic errors begin with small, unconscious distortions that spiral into certainty.

Memory Topic: Change Blindness and Inattentional Blindness
This game explores the phenomenon of change blindness or our surprising inability to notice changes in a visual scene. We assume we perceive the world in detail, but our minds only encode fragments of what we see, based on where we’re focusing. This means large changes can happen right before our eyes and we won’t notice especially if there’s a moment of distraction or a visual flicker.


Real-World Case Study: The Invisible Gorilla Experiment
In a now-famous 1999 study by Simons and Chabris, participants were asked to count basketball passes among players. During the video, a person in a gorilla suit walked through the scene. Astonishingly, about half of the viewers didn't notice the gorilla at all. This phenomenon isn't just a lab curiosity; it has real-world implications. For instance, drivers may fail to notice pedestrians, and security personnel might overlook significant changes in surveillance footage. Emoji Change Blindness gives you a playful but sobering window into this blind spot in our visual perception.
